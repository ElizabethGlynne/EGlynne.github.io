Elizabeth Glynne

Comparative Anatomist & Evolutionary Biologist

PhD Candidate in Ecology and Evolutionary Biology at Iowa State University

I am a comparative anatomist specializing in evolutionary biology, with my research focused on understanding the morphological consequences of evolutionary change. Leveraging my expertise in geckos, I have studied the evolution of novel traits, sexual size dimorphism between closely related groups, and how miniaturization, or the evolution of extremely small body size, influences morphological integration. Through these studies, I aim to uncover how evolutionary processes shape morphological diversity and the functional roles these traits may play. I utilize advanced techniques such as 3D modeling—including CT scanning and photogrammetry—geometric morphometrics, and phylogenetic comparative methods to explore these complex evolutionary processes, providing deeper insights into the mechanisms driving the diversity of life.
