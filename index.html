Key Research Interests: Macroevolutionary patterns of phenotypic and morphological diversity; vertebrate evolution; scaling, body size evolution, and sexual dimorphism; comparative anatomy; geometric morphometrics; phylogenetic comparative methods; and the use of 3D modeling (CT scanning and photogrammetry) to support research and undergraduate teaching.

PhD in Ecology and Evolutionary Biology at Iowa State University

I am a conceptual evolutionary biologist dedicated to understanding how evolutionary and ecological processes shape phenotypic diversity across vertebrates. My research has focused on the evolutionary patterns and consequences of trait changes, such as sexual size dimorphism, miniaturization, and morphological integration. By leveraging advanced technique, such a  geometric morphometrics, CT scanning, photogrammetry, and phylogenetic comparative methods,  I aim to explore how evolutionary forces have driven phenotypic diversity across species, while recognizing the broader ecological and evolutionary contexts in which these traits evolve. This research also informs my teaching, allowing me to integrate real datasets, comparative frameworks, and digital anatomy tools into undergraduate biology courses.
